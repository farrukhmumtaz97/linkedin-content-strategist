# Post Anatomy

How high-performing LinkedIn posts are structured beneath the surface. Read this when analyzing a pasted post or building a complex original.

---

## The five-section frame

Every high-performing LinkedIn post has roughly these five sections, even when the writer didn't consciously plan them.

### 1. Hook (1-3 lines)
Stops the scroll. Creates the gap. See `hook-library.md` for pattern-break techniques.

### 2. Setup (2-5 lines)
Resolves the hook just enough to keep the reader, without giving away the payoff. Establishes stakes, context, or character. This is where most posts lose people. Short paragraphs, fast rhythm, one-sentence beats.

### 3. Body / Story progression (the meat)
The actual insight, story, or argument. Mix sentence lengths. Build through specific observations, not abstractions. Avoid bullet lists unless the structure genuinely demands one.

### 4. Reframe (the turn)
The single line that makes the post matter. It's where the personal becomes universal, the tactical becomes strategic, the small becomes meaningful. Without a reframe, the post is just an anecdote or a how-to. With one, it becomes positioning.

Examples of reframes:
- "Because marketing isn't about content. It's about people."
- "A hook isn't a sentence that grabs attention. It's a sentence the reader can't predict."
- "The job that saves you isn't always the one you wanted."

### 5. Closer / Screenshottable line
The line people quote, screenshot, or steal. Often a callback to the hook. Often shorter than the rest of the post. Often the most carefully written sentence.

## Spacing and rhythm

LinkedIn rewards posts that read fast on mobile.

- **One-sentence paragraphs.** Most beats should be one sentence followed by a blank line.
- **Vary sentence length.** Don't make every line the same length. Three short, one long, one short. Read it out loud — if the rhythm is flat, rebuild.
- **White space is a feature.** A post with no breathing room signals AI or signals "I wrote this in a hurry."
- **Maximum 2-3 lines per "paragraph"** before a break, with most being one line.

## Open loops

Open loops are unresolved questions or implied promises the reader expects to be paid off later in the post.

**Where to plant them:** End of the hook. End of the setup. Just before any list or framework.

**How to plant them:**
- "I learned this the hard way." (loop: what hard way?)
- "It took me three years to figure out why." (loop: figure out what?)
- "Then a restaurant called." (loop: what did the restaurant lead to?)

**The rule:** Every open loop must be paid off. If you plant a loop and never resolve it, the reader feels cheated and won't share.

## Tension and release

The best posts feel like a small emotional journey, even tactical ones.

- **Tension** comes from stakes, conflict, or contradiction.
- **Release** comes from the reframe or the payoff.
- The space between them is where attention lives.

A post with tension but no release feels frustrating. A post with release but no tension feels obvious. You need both.

## Section-by-section analysis framework

Use this when analyzing a pasted post.

### Hook analysis questions
- Which pattern-break technique is used?
- Is there a curiosity gap?
- What's the implicit promise?
- Could this hook be swapped into another post without anyone noticing?

### Setup analysis questions
- Does the second paragraph deliver enough to keep the reader without resolving the gap?
- Is the pacing fast enough for mobile?
- Are stakes or character established?

### Body analysis questions
- Where is the reader most likely to drop off? Usually around the 60% mark.
- How does the writer recover attention there? Often with a short, punchy line or a fresh angle.
- Are observations specific or abstract?

### Reframe analysis questions
- Is there a single line that elevates the post from anecdote to insight?
- Does the reframe connect to the writer's professional brand?
- Is it stated, or is it implied?

### Closer analysis questions
- Is there a screenshottable line?
- Does the closer call back to the hook?
- Does it end with a question, statement, or implied CTA?

## How to handle the CTA

LinkedIn posts that explicitly ask for engagement ("What do you think? Comment below!") feel desperate. Strong posts generate engagement through:

- **An implied question.** A reframe that the reader naturally wants to respond to.
- **A divisive opinion.** Half the audience will agree, half will push back. Both comment.
- **A specific detail people want to validate or contradict.** "I've grown three accounts past 100K. This is the pattern I see every time."

**The soft CTA scale, from strongest to weakest:**

1. No CTA. The post itself is the CTA. (Best for personal stories and reframes.)
2. An implied question in the closer.
3. A genuine open question at the end ("Curious if anyone else has seen this.")
4. An explicit ask ("Comment what you think.") — avoid.
5. A multi-part ask ("Like, comment, follow.") — never.

## The two-thirds rule

If you cut the bottom two-thirds of your post, the top should still be interesting enough to read. If it isn't, the top is doing too little work.

If you cut the top two-thirds, the bottom should still feel like the conclusion of a thought. If it doesn't, the bottom is doing too little work.

Apply this rule to every draft.

## Length

- **Short posts (under 600 chars):** Punchy opinions. Single-thought posts. Higher engagement rate but lower reach.
- **Medium posts (600-1,500 chars):** Most personal stories and reframes. Sweet spot for shareability.
- **Long posts (1,500-3,000 chars):** Tactical breakdowns, founder reflections. Strong for saves but harder to maintain attention.
- **Anything over 3,000 chars:** Almost always too long. Either cut or split into a carousel.

The skill should default to medium length unless the topic genuinely demands more.

## What makes a post "human"

- Sentence length variation that doesn't follow a rule
- One detail that's slightly too specific to be invented
- A line that's slightly imperfect grammatically but rhythmically right
- A small admission of uncertainty or imperfection
- An observation that feels earned, not researched
- A closer that doesn't try too hard

## What makes a post feel AI-generated

- Every paragraph the same length
- Inflated symbolism ("a tapestry," "navigating," "landscape of")
- Three-item parallel lists used mechanically
- Em-dashes everywhere
- "It's worth noting" / "At the end of the day" / "In today's"
- Symmetric "not X, but Y" used more than once
- A closer that reads like the AI thinks it's deep

The humanizer pass in `SKILL.md` catches most of these. But the structural fix is rhythm variation.

## When the post isn't working

Common failure modes and fixes:

| Symptom | Likely cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Hook is fine but readers don't continue | Setup is too slow or too abstract | Cut the setup in half. Use a scene or specific detail. |
| Body feels flat | No tension or no specifics | Add one specific number, name, or moment. Or add a contradiction. |
| Reader gets to the end but doesn't engage | No reframe | Add one line that elevates the post above its surface topic. |
| Reads as preachy | Direct address used too much | Pull back to observation. Talk about the pattern, not the reader. |
| Reads as AI | Symmetric rhythm + inflated language | Cut every adjective. Vary sentence length. Read out loud. |
| Reads as a humble brag | Credential dropped too early | Move the credibility anchor to mid-post or remove it entirely. |
