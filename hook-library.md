# Hook Library

Pattern-break techniques for opening LinkedIn posts. Each technique has psychological reasoning so the skill picks the right one for the post, not just the first one that fits.

A hook's job isn't to grab attention. It's to make scrolling feel like the wrong decision. The brain skips anything it's seen before. Hooks fail when they're predictable, not when they're boring.

**Rule:** Under 80 words. Curiosity in the first three words ideally. Conversational. Native to LinkedIn. Never clickbait.

---

## 1. The Confession Pattern Break

Open with a small admission of something most LinkedIn users wouldn't admit. Imperfection, mistake, ignorance, or doubt.

**Why it works:** LinkedIn is a performance space. A small confession is a pattern break because most posts are performing competence. Confessions buy you immediate credibility for everything that follows.

**Examples (for reference, do not copy):**
- "I deleted 47 posts last year before publishing them."
- "I moved to the UK with no job, no house, and no idea how rent worked."
- "I've been wrong about hooks for two years."

**When to use:** Personal stories. Vulnerability posts. Pieces where the user is reframing something they used to believe.

---

## 2. The Specificity Pattern Break

Open with a number that has no context, forcing the reader's brain to need the next line to make sense of the first.

**Why it works:** The brain is wired to resolve incomplete information. A naked number creates a curiosity gap that physically pulls the reader to the next line.

**Examples:**
- "47 percent."
- "9,000 LinkedIn posts in six months."
- "Three hours. That's how long it took."

**When to use:** Tactical posts, breakdown posts, or any post where a specific number is the spine of the argument.

**Warning:** The number must be real or clearly framed as illustrative. Never invent stats.

---

## 3. The Contradiction Pattern Break

Open with a sentence that directly contradicts a widely held belief in the industry.

**Why it works:** Contradiction creates cognitive friction the brain must resolve before it can scroll. It also signals "I'm about to say something the other 80% of posts won't."

**Examples:**
- "Good hooks don't grab attention."
- "AI didn't kill creativity. It exposed who never had it."
- "Your content isn't bad. It's invisible."

**When to use:** Contrarian takes, opinion posts, industry critique. Must be backed by real reasoning in the body or the post collapses.

---

## 4. The Observer Pattern Break

Open by positioning yourself as someone who watches the field carefully, not someone performing in it.

**Why it works:** People trust observers more than experts on LinkedIn right now. Expert framing triggers skepticism. Observer framing triggers curiosity.

**Examples:**
- "I've read about 9,000 LinkedIn posts in the last six months."
- "I've watched 200 founders launch products this year."
- "Roughly 80% of marketing advice on this platform is recycled from people who've never tested it."

**When to use:** Authority posts, industry analysis, posts where the user wants to position themselves as the smart person in the room without bragging.

---

## 5. The Direct Address Pattern Break

Open with a sentence aimed at the reader, not about the topic.

**Why it works:** Direct address activates the reader's identity. The brain processes "you" sentences differently than third-person sentences. It pulls them in personally before they've decided to engage.

**Examples:**
- "Your hook isn't bad. It's invisible. That's worse."
- "If you're using AI to write your posts, your readers can tell."
- "The reason your content isn't growing isn't what you think it is."

**When to use:** Bold opinion posts, critique posts, posts where you're confronting a behavior the audience is doing wrong. Use sparingly. Overuse turns into preaching.

---

## 6. The Scene Pattern Break

Open with a small, specific moment that physically locates the reader in time and place.

**Why it works:** Scenes activate visualization. The reader can see the moment. Once they're seeing it, they're committed to finding out what happens next.

**Examples:**
- "One Friday night I was sitting in a shared flat in a new city, refreshing my email."
- "It was 11pm on a Tuesday and I'd just refreshed analytics for the 30th time."
- "The first time I saw 1 million views on a single post, I was eating instant noodles."

**When to use:** Personal stories. Wednesday posts. Founder reflection posts. Any time emotional resonance matters more than analytical sharpness.

---

## 7. The Unexpected Comparison Pattern Break

Open by comparing something familiar to something unfamiliar, in a way that reframes both.

**Why it works:** Unexpected comparisons create instant intellectual delight. The reader's brain rewards itself for "getting it." That small dopamine hit keeps them reading.

**Examples:**
- "Most LinkedIn hooks are just clickbait with a marketing degree."
- "Your content strategy is a Wikipedia article when it should be a group chat."
- "AI-generated copy reads like an intern who's never been outside."

**When to use:** Opinion posts where humor matters. Posts about the industry, AI, or content trends. Use one comparison max per post. Multiple comparisons make the post feel try-hard.

---

## 8. The Question-as-Statement Pattern Break

Open with a question that isn't really asking. It's asserting.

**Why it works:** Questions trigger reflection. Reflection slows scrolling. But the question must feel earned, not rhetorical.

**Examples:**
- "When did everyone on LinkedIn start sounding the same?"
- "What if your audience isn't ignoring your posts because they're bad, but because they're forgettable?"
- "Why does every AI-written post end with three rhetorical questions?"

**When to use:** Sparingly. Maximum once a month. Question hooks are overused on LinkedIn and the bar is high. Only use when the question itself reframes something.

---

## 9. The Confession of a Common Mistake

Open by admitting you used to do the thing the post is critiquing.

**Why it works:** Critique without prior implication reads as superiority. Confession of having made the same mistake creates earned authority. The reader doesn't feel lectured. They feel companioned.

**Examples:**
- "I used to think hooks were the most important part of a post. I was wrong."
- "For two years I wrote captions like a press release. Nobody told me they were terrible."
- "I posted four times a week for a year and built nothing."

**When to use:** Educational posts. Founder lessons. Anywhere you want to teach without seeming preachy.

---

## How to choose

| Post type | Best hook type |
|---|---|
| Personal story (Wednesday) | Confession, Scene |
| Authority/Insight (Monday) | Contradiction, Observer, Unexpected Comparison |
| Tactical/Educational | Specificity, Confession of Common Mistake |
| Contrarian/Debate (Thursday) | Contradiction, Direct Address |
| Founder Reflection (Friday) | Confession, Scene, Confession of Common Mistake |
| AI/Industry commentary | Contradiction, Observer, Unexpected Comparison |

## What hooks should never do

- Start with "In today's world"
- Promise something the post can't deliver
- Use "You won't believe what happened"
- Lead with a credential ("As a marketer with 10 years experience...")
- Open with a quote from someone else
- Use a stat without context that the reader can't verify
- Try to be funny before the reader has decided to read

## The screenshottable line

The hook gets people in. The screenshottable line — usually near the end — is what makes them share. Every post should have one. Aim for one line that could stand alone on a graphic and still feel true.

Examples:
- "It's a headline pretending."
- "The job that saves you isn't always the one you wanted."
- "AI didn't kill creativity. It exposed who never had it."

If you can't identify the screenshottable line in a draft, the post isn't done.
