# CTA Library

How to end LinkedIn posts so they convert without killing reach. Most LinkedIn posts end with either nothing or "What do you think? Comment below!" Both are wrong. The first wastes the moment of highest reader attention. The second flags the post as engagement-bait and tanks algorithmic reach.

The skill should pick one CTA per post from the three tiers below, based on post type, recency of previous direct CTAs, and where the reader is in the funnel.

## The three-tier CTA system

| Tier | Goal | Use frequency | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Soft** | Engagement (comments, saves, reshares) | 60-70% of posts | An implied question, a divisive line, a "curious if anyone else has seen this" |
| **Medium** | Conversation / inbound (DMs, replies) | 20-30% of posts | "DM me X if Y," "reply with Y," "tell me Y in the comments" |
| **Direct** | Conversion (newsletter, link, lead magnet) | 10-15% of posts maximum | Newsletter signup link, landing page, gated resource |

**Rotation rule:** Never run two direct CTAs in a row. After a direct CTA, the next two posts must be soft or medium. This protects reach and prevents reader fatigue.

**The 1-in-7 rule:** If posting daily, only one post per week should carry a direct CTA. For Mon/Wed/Fri cadence, one direct CTA per 7-10 posts is the sweet spot. The rest carry soft or medium CTAs.

## Tier 1: Soft CTAs (most common)

The post itself is the CTA. The reader doesn't realize they're being asked for anything. Engagement happens because the post creates a natural urge to respond.

### Soft CTA pattern A: The implied question
End on a reframe or observation that the reader naturally wants to weigh in on. No question mark needed. The line itself is the prompt.

Examples:
- "If your first line could be swapped into someone else's post without anyone noticing, it's not a hook. It's a headline pretending."
- "The job that saves you isn't always the one you wanted."
- "LinkedIn just shows your bad content to more people, faster."

**Why it works:** The brain auto-generates a response. Some readers will comment to agree, some to push back, some to add their own version. All three are engagement.

### Soft CTA pattern B: The divisive line
End with a statement that splits the audience cleanly in half. The disagreement IS the engagement.

Examples:
- "Most marketers will hate this take. The good ones already know it's true."
- "If you needed me to tell you this, you've been on LinkedIn too long."
- "AI didn't kill creativity. It exposed who never had it."

**Why it works:** Half the audience comments to defend their position. The other half comments to validate yours. Algorithm reads both as engagement.

### Soft CTA pattern C: The genuine open question
A real question the writer is curious about, asked in a way that invites perspective, not validation.

Examples:
- "Curious if anyone else has watched this play out."
- "Anyone testing this differently? Curious what's working."
- "I don't know if this is just me. Anyone seeing the same pattern?"

**Why it works:** Genuine curiosity reads differently than performative engagement bait. Readers can tell the difference. Use sparingly — overuse turns it into a tic.

**When to use Tier 1:** Default for Monday authority posts, Friday opinion posts, contrarian takes, and most short-form content. Default for anyone with under 5,000 followers (need to build reach before converting it).

## Tier 2: Medium CTAs (20-30% of posts)

A specific, low-friction ask. Not "buy something." Just "engage in a more meaningful way." These build the inbound conversation pipeline.

### Medium CTA pattern A: The DM trigger
Invite a DM only if the reader meets a specific condition. The specificity prevents inbox spam and signals that you actually want to talk.

Examples:
- "If you've grown an account past 100K, I'd love to compare notes. DM me."
- "Working on a similar problem? Send me a message, happy to share what's worked."
- "If this is happening at your company too, drop me a DM. Want to test a theory."

**Why it works:** The condition filters for quality. Random readers don't DM. The ones who do are pre-qualified leads or genuine peers.

### Medium CTA pattern B: The reply prompt
Ask the reader to reply to the post itself with something specific and low-effort.

Examples:
- "What's one thing you've changed your mind about this year? Curious."
- "Reply with one piece of advice you'd give your younger self about social media."
- "Drop a number 1-10 for how much you agree with this."

**Why it works:** Specific prompts get more replies than generic ones. "Drop a number" beats "What do you think?" because it removes the cognitive load of crafting a response.

### Medium CTA pattern C: The micro-resource offer
Offer something small in exchange for engagement. No links, no friction. The resource is delivered via DM or comment.

Examples:
- "I made a list of the 12 hooks that worked best for me last year. Want it? Say 'hooks' in the comments and I'll send it over."
- "Got a template I use for content audits. Comment 'audit' and I'll send the link."

**Why it works:** Comment-and-receive CTAs generate massive comment volume, which the algorithm rewards. But use sparingly — feels gimmicky if overused. Maximum once a month.

**When to use Tier 2:** Posts where genuine conversation matters more than reach. Educational posts. Founder reflection posts. Any post where you want to identify the high-intent readers in the audience.

## Tier 3: Direct CTAs (10-15% of posts, never more)

The post explicitly drives traffic somewhere external — usually a newsletter, landing page, or scheduling link. Direct CTAs cost reach because the algorithm deprioritizes posts that push readers off-platform. Worth it occasionally; deadly if overused.

### Direct CTA pattern A: The relevance bridge
The post itself naturally builds to the resource. The CTA feels like a logical next step, not a sales pitch.

Example structure:
```
[Post body about why most newsletter forms don't convert]

I write about this kind of thing every Friday.
One email. One real breakdown. No fluff.
Link in the first comment if you want it.
```

**Why it works:** The reader is already in the headspace the resource speaks to. The CTA is presented as additional value, not a pivot.

### Direct CTA pattern B: The casual mention
Drop the resource as an aside, mid-post or near the end. No "click here" energy.

Example:
```
This is the kind of breakdown I do in my Friday email if you want more.
[link]
```

**Why it works:** Reads like a friend mentioning a side project, not a marketer making an ask. Lower conversion than pattern A but higher trust.

### Direct CTA pattern C: The pinned-comment redirect
Don't put the link in the post itself. Put it in the first comment. Mention the comment in the post.

Example:
```
If you want the full version, link's in the first comment.
```

**Why it works:** LinkedIn's algorithm penalizes posts with external links in the main body. Comment links bypass this. Reach stays intact. Conversion only slightly lower.

**When to use Tier 3:**
- Maximum 1 in 7-10 posts.
- Best after a strong week of engagement (algorithm credit accumulated).
- Best when the post topic naturally bridges to the resource.
- Never on Wednesday personal stories (kills the emotional moment).
- Never two direct CTAs in a row.

## The CTA decision tree

For every post the skill generates, walk through this in order:

1. **Is this a Wednesday personal story?**
   - Yes → Soft CTA only (Pattern A or B). Direct CTAs destroy emotional resonance. Skip to step 5.

2. **What was the last post's CTA tier?**
   - Direct → This post must be Soft.
   - Medium → This post can be Soft or Medium, not Direct.
   - Soft → This post can be Soft, Medium, or Direct.
   - Unknown → Default to Soft.

3. **Does the post topic naturally bridge to a resource?**
   - Yes + last CTA wasn't Direct + topic genuinely connects → Direct is on the table.
   - No → Soft or Medium only.

4. **Pick the tier, then pick the pattern:**
   - Tier chosen → Match the pattern to the post's emotional tone. Don't use a divisive line CTA on a vulnerable personal story. Don't use a DM trigger on a quick observation post.

5. **Write the CTA. Then check: does it sound forced?**
   - If yes → rewrite or drop to a softer tier. A forced CTA is worse than no CTA.

## What NEVER appears in any CTA

- "Like, comment, and share!"
- "What do you think? Drop a comment below!"
- "Tag someone who needs to see this!"
- "Repost to help others!"
- Emojis pointing down ↓ to the CTA
- "P.S." (signals AI/template energy)
- Multiple CTAs in one post
- A link in the main body of the post (always in the first comment)
- A CTA that doesn't connect to the post's actual topic

These all signal "I read a LinkedIn growth thread once." They tank reach and credibility.

## CTA examples by post type

### Monday Authority post
- **Default:** Soft (implied question via the reframe line)
- **Sometimes:** Medium (DM trigger if the post asks a specific tactical question)
- **Rarely:** Direct (only if the post topic IS the newsletter topic)

### Wednesday Personal Story
- **Always:** Soft (implied question or quiet reframe)
- **Never:** Direct (kills the emotional payoff)
- **Sometimes:** Medium reply prompt, only if it extends the story naturally

### Friday Vision / Industry Take
- **Default:** Soft (divisive line)
- **Sometimes:** Medium (DM trigger, especially "I'd love to compare notes")
- **Sometimes:** Direct (this is the day with the most flexibility for newsletter promotion)

### Tactical/Educational (Tuesday if user posts 5x/week)
- **Default:** Medium (micro-resource offer or reply prompt)
- **Sometimes:** Direct (if the post is essentially a teaser for a longer resource)

### Contrarian/Debate (Thursday if user posts 5x/week)
- **Always:** Soft (divisive line — let the disagreement BE the engagement)
- **Never:** Direct (mixing controversy with a sales ask reads cynically)

## How to embed a direct CTA gracefully

When the post warrants a direct CTA (and only then), the placement and phrasing matter more than the link itself.

### Phrasing examples that work

- "I send one of these every Friday. Link in the first comment if you want it."
- "I write a Friday breakdown like this one. [link in comment] if you're into it."
- "The full version is in my Friday email. Link below."
- "If you want more of these, I send one every Friday. First comment has the link."

### Phrasing that doesn't work

- "Sign up for my newsletter!" (generic, no specifics)
- "Click here to subscribe!" (begging)
- "Don't miss out — join 1,000+ readers!" (FOMO that reads desperate)
- "Get exclusive content!" (vague, dated)

### The link itself

**Always put external links in the first comment, not the post body.** LinkedIn's algorithm penalizes posts with external links in the main body — visibility drops by an estimated 30-50%. Comment links don't trigger the penalty.

Format the comment link clean:
```
Newsletter link if you want it:
[paste URL]
```

No emojis, no "👇 Click here 👇" energy. The CTA in the post did the asking. The comment just delivers.
